## L06: Discussion - Programming Languages

**Fundamental Differences: Compare and contrast the fundamental characteristics of machine, assembly, and high-level programming languages. What are the main differences in terms of syntax, abstraction, and proximity to hardware?**

When it comes to machine language, its syntax is simply binary (0s and 1s), it has no abstraction as it's the hardware's native tongue, and it has the most direct proximity to hardware. It also has no protability, being specific to the processor. Assembly starts to get easier to actually be more interactive, it's syntax is mnemonic codes, it has low abstraction as it acts as a thin wrapper over macine code, and it has a very close proximity to hardware but requires the programmer to still have 1:1 understanding of the underlying CPU. It's portability is low but not 0. High-level languages offer the most flexibility and portability as it can run on various platforms with minimal changes. It also has the lowest proximity to hardware, as that part is handled with compilers and interpreters.

**Use Cases: Explore the domains and scenarios where each type of programming language is commonly used. How does the choice of language relate to the specific tasks or applications being developed? Provide examples to illustrate your points.**

As for use cases, it depends on the required performance of a system and the environment it is being developed in. When it comes to machine code and assembly, they are generally used for embedded systems (like microcontrollers), and developers need to manipulate a specific hardware register or optimize every bit of code for extreme efficiency.

For mid-level languages (think C or C++), these are generally used for things like game engines or operating systems. Here, performance is still a big factor, but these systems need high abstraction and must support complex logic that is almost impossible to do with just assembly alone.

High level languages (Python, Java) are used for most of the applications you interact with on a daily basis. Think web apps, enterprise software, and data science applications as well. This is the most accesible level for people to write software.

## L09: Discussion- The Internet and the Web

The terms "The Internet" and "The World Wide Web (The Web)" are often used interchangeably, and for good reason. These terms are deeply connected and it makes sense to use either one in most contexts for casual conversations. However, there are differences. Think of the internet as the overarching technology and the web as a service that operates on top of it. The internet itself is like a massive, global network of networks, where it refers to the physical and logical infastructure that maintains it. It consists of millions of private, public, academic, government, and business networks. The web is a collection of information that is if often organized into documents or other forms, and are linked by URLS and hyperlinks.


**Underlying technologies**

There are a few key technologies that work under the hood to keep the internet running. The first of which are "Protocols". These essentially are the rules the internet abides by. For example, TCP/IP (Transmission Control Protocol/Internet Protocol) makes sure the data that is traveling across the internet is broken into packets and delivered correctly across the Internet. On the Web, HTTP/HTTPS (Hypertext Transfer Protocol) dictates how messages are formatted and transmitted between web servers and browsers. Servers are powerful computers that serves up content when requested, and a broswer is how most ineract with the internet. They are client-side software and requests the files from a server, which gets interpreted as code, and is then rendered on the browser into a readable page. Hyperlinks are like the glue of the web, they allow users to hop acorss different sites and create non-linear and interconnected webs across the internet.

## L13: Discussion- Crowdsourcing

**Ethical Considerations: Discuss ethical aspects of crowdsourcing, including compensation for contributors, protection of intellectual property, and ensuring privacy and data security.**

When it comes to the ethics on this topic, the most pressing matter here is on fair compensation. While I do agree that it is an amazing thing that crowdsourcing allows us to leverage the collective intelligence of many people, it can also just as easily exploit their contributions and not compensate them fairly. For example, lets say a large company opens up one of their problems for crowdsourcing, but then only rewards the top contributors without acknowledging others who made contributions as well, then that becomes lopsided in how evenly the reward was distributed for the task. While it is true that they can simply claim that only x percent of their contributors will get a prize, this type of model can easily be exploited. Another grey area here is on how Intellectual Property is handled, because if something is opened for crowdsourcing, the party that opened their IP up must accept that they leave their IP open to being used.
